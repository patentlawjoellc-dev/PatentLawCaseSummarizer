## Universal Electronics, Inc. v. Roku, Inc.

- Tags: non-precedential, standing / jurisdiction, waiver / forfeiture, remand, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1856.ORDER.4-23-2026_2681815.pdf

**Key points:**

- The parties stipulated to voluntary dismissal of Roku's appeal (24-1916) under Fed. R. App. P. 42(b), with each side bearing its own costs.
- The Federal Circuit issued the mandate as to Appeal No. 24-1916 immediately on April 23, 2026.
- Universal Electronics' appeal (24-1856) is unaffected and remains pending before the Federal Circuit.
- This is a nonprecedential administrative order with no substantive ruling on the merits of the underlying IPR.

## Slingshot Printing LLC v. Canon U.S.A., Inc.

- Appeal: 24-1956
- Origin: PTO
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: § 103, claim construction, non-precedential, remand, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1956.OPINION.4-23-2026_2681303.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's finding that claims 1–5 and 8 of the '341 patent are unpatentable as obvious over Torgerson and Bruce, holding that the Board's findings on the polysilicon conductor limitation, motivation to combine, and remaining claim limitations were each supported by substantial evidence and satisfied APA explanation requirements.

**Why it matters:** Confirms that a Board obviousness decision survives APA scrutiny even where the written decision incorporates portions of the petition by reference with citations, so long as the Board's reasoning path is reasonably discernible—and distinguishes Virtek by emphasizing that finite-solutions and design-need evidence can supply the motivation-to-combine showing Virtek found lacking.

**Key points:**
- Polysilicon conductor limitation: The Board credited Canon's expert that a skilled artisan would understand Torgerson's polysilicon gate fingers necessarily extend to its logic circuits; the Federal Circuit found substantial evidence supported that reading and rejected Slingshot's argument that Canon improperly recharacterized the Board's decision or relied on an undisclosed inherency theory.
- Motivation to combine: Both Torgerson and Bruce are in the same field (inkjet printhead semiconductor substrates); the Board credited evidence of design needs (substrate size reduction), market pressures, and a finite number of known, predictable solutions—distinguishing Virtek, where the record lacked any such evidence.
- APA adequacy: The Board's decision on remaining limitations satisfied the APA because it identified the arguments and evidence reviewed, cited the relevant petition sections, and stated its conclusion; the Federal Circuit found the reasoning path reasonably discernible under Bowman Transport.
- Standard of review: Obviousness legal conclusion reviewed de novo; underlying factual findings—including prior-art teachings and motivation to combine—reviewed for substantial evidence.
- Nonprecedential: The opinion should not be cited as binding authority, though its application of Virtek and the APA-adequacy analysis is instructive for IPR practitioners.

## Network-1 Technologies, Inc. v. Google LLC

- Appeal: 24-1893
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded.
- Tags: § 112(b), claim construction, infringement, remand, non-precedential, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1893.OPINION.4-23-2026_2681375.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed indefiniteness of the '988 and '464 patent claims on the term 'non-exhaustive search' and affirmed summary judgment of noninfringement of the '237 patent as to the LSH version of Content ID, but reversed summary judgment of noninfringement as to the Siberia version of Content ID, finding a genuine issue of material fact on whether Siberia's 'tunable knobs' and an internal Google document stating it 'will need a sublinear search' satisfy the 'sublinear approximate nearest neighbor search' limitation.

**Why it matters:** The decision illustrates that claim terms added years after filing to overcome prior art—without clear definitional support in the specification—face serious indefiniteness risk, and that a single internal design document stating a system 'will need' a claimed feature can be enough to survive summary judgment of noninfringement.

**Key points:**
- Indefiniteness ('988 and '464 patents): The term 'non-exhaustive search' was added to the claims nine years after the provisional filing and three years after the patent filing date to overcome prior art, but the specification never uses the phrase and does not draw a clear line between 'exhaustive' and 'non-exhaustive' searching in terms of how much data must be considered—neither intrinsic nor extrinsic evidence (including Network-1's expert declaration and academic papers) resolved the ambiguity.
- Prior IPR analysis informative but not binding: The court noted that its earlier BRI-standard construction of 'non-exhaustive search' in the IPR appeal (vacating the Board) was instructive on the written-description analysis even though the Phillips standard applies in district court, because the specification's deficiency is standard-agnostic.
- Summary judgment affirmed as to LSH version: Network-1's evidence—Baluja research papers, a 2010 Google draft document, and an expert report—all failed to connect to the actual implementation of the LSH Content ID system; the draft document contemplated both sublinear and non-sublinear LSH tables without evidence of which was built, and the expert report merely walked through the code without analyzing sublinearity.
- Summary judgment reversed as to Siberia version: Two pieces of evidence created a triable issue—(1) evidence that Siberia's 'tunable knobs' allowed the system to adapt execution time to a growing dataset in less than a proportional relationship, and (2) an internal Google document stating Siberia 'will need a sublinear search,' which a factfinder could read as imposing a sublinear requirement on any Siberia implementation.
- Practical takeaway on expert reports: The court reiterated that an expert opinion that merely describes how a system works without analyzing why it satisfies the disputed claim limitation is insufficient to defeat summary judgment.

## NantWorks, LLC v. Niantic, Inc.

- Appeal: 24-2216
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: § 101, claim construction, non-precedential, remand
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-2216.OPINION.4-23-2026_2681364.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed that asserted claims of both the '518 patent (directed to mapping and rendering AR objects based on device location using tessellated tile maps) and the '051 patent (directed to selecting and altering AR objects based on device location and context) are patent-ineligible under § 101 as directed to abstract ideas with no inventive concept. Under Alice/Mayo step one, the '518 claims are directed to 'providing information based on a location on a map' and the '051 claims to 'receiving information about a location and displaying materials based on that information'; under step two, neither patent's claims supply an inventive concept beyond those abstract ideas.

**Why it matters:** Reinforces that AR-specific claim language—tessellated tiles, virtual element attributes, altered AR object presence—does not rescue location-based information-display claims from § 101 invalidity when the underlying concept remains providing or filtering information based on location, and that unclaimed specification benefits cannot substitute for an inventive concept in the claims themselves.

**Key points:**
- Alice/Mayo step one: Both patents' claims reduce to location-based information filtering and display—an abstract idea—regardless of AR-specific terminology; limiting an abstract idea to the AR field of use does not confer eligibility.
- Alice/Mayo step two: The court rejected NantWorks's 'selective memory population' argument for the '518 patent because the word 'selectively' does not appear in the claims; unclaimed specification advantages cannot supply an inventive concept.
- For the '051 patent, the court found NantWorks's expert testimony (Dr. Turk) insufficient to create a genuine factual dispute at step two because the cited paragraphs were either conclusory, repeated unclaimed specification passages, or described abstract benefits rather than identifying an inventive concept within the claims.
- The 'pencil and paper' test applied to '518 claim 7: each claimed step (locate on map, identify relevant area, display content as overlay) could be performed manually, confirming abstraction.
- This is a nonprecedential disposition; readers should treat it as illustrative of how the court applies existing § 101 doctrine to AR/location-based patents rather than as binding authority.

## Centripetal Networks, LLC v. ITC

- Appeal: 24-1416
- Origin: ITC
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: domestic industry, infringement, waiver / forfeiture, claim construction, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1416.OPINION.4-23-2026_2681340.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed the ITC's finding of no Section 337 violation as to the '370 Patent, holding that substantial evidence supported the Commission's determination that Centripetal's domestic industry product failed to satisfy the technical prong of the domestic industry requirement, and that Centripetal waived its challenges to the Commission's non-infringement findings by failing to address key claim limitations in its opening brief.

**Why it matters:** Reinforces that ITC complainants must fully brief every appeal-dispositive infringement limitation in their opening brief or risk waiver, and that a domestic industry product must actually practice each claim limitation—not merely a functional approximation of the claimed 'device.'

**Key points:**
- The '917 Patent issues were mooted by the Federal Circuit's same-day affirmance of unpatentability in No. 24-1406, leaving only the '370 Patent in dispute.
- On the technical prong, the Commission found—and the Federal Circuit agreed on substantial evidence review—that Centripetal's RuleGATE ports are passive electrical interfaces that cannot be 'provisioned with rules' or 'generate log entries' as required by claim 22(b)–(f); the appliance itself, not the ports, stores and applies rules.
- On infringement, Centripetal waived its challenge to the Commission's findings on the 'identify' and 'communicate' aspects of limitations (e) and (f) of claims 22 and 43 by failing to address those specific findings in its opening brief.
- Centripetal also waived its limitation (h) 'communicate' argument on appeal by failing to address the Commission's own below-level waiver finding on that sub-element.
- Because both the domestic industry technical prong failure and the infringement waivers independently supported affirmance, the court declined to reach the patent ineligibility ground.

## Centripetal Networks, LLC v. Keysight Technologies, Inc.

- Appeal: 2024-1406, 2024-1473
- Origin: PTO
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed in part, reversed in part. Claims 1–3, 5–13, and 15–20 remain unpatentable; claims 4 and 14 are now also unpatentable for obviousness.
- Tags: § 103, claim construction, remand, non-precedential, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1406.OPINION.4-23-2026_2681317.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's obviousness findings as to claims 1–3, 5–13, and 15–20, holding that substantial evidence supported the Board's claim construction of 'packet flow entry' and its finding that Sourcefire (alone or combined with Macaulay) disclosed the relevant limitations. The court reversed as to claims 4 and 14, holding that the Board's non-obviousness determination lacked substantial evidence support because the Board improperly read a 'continuous updating' requirement into the claims and its subsidiary factual findings regarding Sourcefire's disclosures were internally inconsistent and unsupported by the record.

**Why it matters:** The decision illustrates that PTAB factual findings on obviousness must be internally consistent across related claims—a Board cannot find that prior art discloses a feature sufficient for one claim while simultaneously finding the same feature absent for a dependent claim without a principled distinction, and importing a 'continuous' limitation from a specification example without clear claim support is reversible error.

**Key points:**
- Claim construction / notice: The Board did not violate APA notice requirements by construing 'packet flow entry' rather than 'packet flow' because Centripetal itself proffered a meaning for 'packet flow entry' in its briefing and was directly questioned on the term at the oral hearing; the Board may adopt a construction neither party proposes without running afoul of the APA.
- Claim construction / substance: The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's construction of 'packet flow entry' as an entry reflecting packets sharing a common feature (e.g., a common threat ID), not limited to packets from the same connection between two endpoints, because the '917 Patent's own specification (Figures 5G and 6G) shows a single entry aggregating packets from multiple distinct connections.
- Obviousness / claims 1–3, 5–13, 15–20: Substantial evidence supported the Board's findings that Sourcefire discloses packet flow analysis data, a device that updates a packet flow entry based on packet log entries, and packet time data; Centripetal's arguments merely invited the court to draw a contrary inference from the same record, which is insufficient to overcome the substantial evidence standard.
- Obviousness / claims 4 and 14 (cross-appeal): The Board erred on three independent grounds—(1) its finding that Sourcefire lacks an 'existing flow log entry' was internally inconsistent with its claim-1 finding that Sourcefire's refresh updates a flow log entry; (2) it improperly imported a 'continuous updating' requirement into claims 4 and 14 from a non-limiting specification example; and (3) it ignored Keysight's petition evidence (including expert testimony and annotated screenshots) showing Sourcefire discloses modification of a flow log entry to indicate corresponding packet-filtering rules and packet disposition.
- Reversal standard: The court reversed outright (rather than remanding) on claims 4 and 14 because only one answer was supported by substantial evidence and there was no apparent reason to grant a second record-making opportunity, consistent with Corning v. Fast Felt Corp.
