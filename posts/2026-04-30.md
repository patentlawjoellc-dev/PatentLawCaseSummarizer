# Federal Circuit Patent Opinions - 2026-04-30

## Kamdem-Ouaffo v. Leblon

- Appeal: 2026-1381
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Order (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Transferred to the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit.
- Tags: standing / jurisdiction, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/26-1381.ORDER.4-30-2026_2685409.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit lacks jurisdiction because the appeal does not arise under the patent laws or any other basis conferring Federal Circuit jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1295. The matter is transferred to the Third Circuit pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1631.

**Why it matters:** A reminder that the Federal Circuit's appellate jurisdiction is narrow—a case touching on trade secrets or other non-patent claims, even filed by a pro se litigant invoking patent-adjacent issues, will be transferred rather than dismissed if the wrong circuit is invoked.

**Key points:**
- Appellant sued attorneys and a New Jersey state-court judge arising from a prior unsuccessful trade secret litigation; no patent claims were at issue.
- The Third Circuit had already affirmed dismissal; this appeal concerned only the district court's subsequent denial of post-judgment relief and recusal motions.
- Federal Circuit jurisdiction under § 1295(a)(1) requires the case to 'arise under' the patent laws—a threshold appellant failed to satisfy.
- Rather than dismiss for lack of jurisdiction, the court transferred to the Third Circuit under 28 U.S.C. § 1631 as the proper circuit.
- Nonprecedential order by Judges Lourie, Chen, and Stark, per curiam.

## Wildseed Mobile, LLC v. Google LLC

- Appeal: 24-1846
- Origin: PTO
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: § 103, claim construction, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1846.OPINION.4-30-2026_2685384.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's construction of 'taken … from a message' to include information generated or derived from message text—not merely verbatim extraction—and affirmed the Board's finding that all challenged claims are unpatentable as obvious. The court also rejected Wildseed's APA challenge to the Board's obviousness analysis of the dependent claims.

**Why it matters:** Patent owners cannot narrow 'taken from a message' to verbatim extraction absent clear lexicography or disclaimer; prosecution history that narrows only the source of information does not also limit the method of obtaining that information.

**Key points:**
- Claim construction: 'taken … from a message' encompasses information derived or generated from message text (e.g., inferring 'golf' from a reference to 'Pebble Beach'), not just verbatim extraction; the claim language limits the source but not the method of obtaining information.
- Prosecution history: The applicant's amendment adding the 'from a text message' limitation distinguished Owensby only on the source of information and the absence of an affirmative user act—it said nothing about verbatim-only extraction, so no disclaimer of derived information.
- Specification: Multiple modules in the preferred embodiments are described as 'generating,' 'analyzing,' and 'determining' information; the court declined to import a verbatim-extraction limitation from Figure 8 into the claims.
- Dependent claims: Because claim 1 is not limited to a single source or verbatim extraction, dependent claims requiring that 'provided information additionally includes' other data types are not so limited either; Wildseed conceded substantial evidence supported obviousness under the correct construction.
- APA: The Board satisfied APA notice-and-comment requirements by quoting the parties' positions, citing intrinsic evidence, and explaining its agreement with Google's construction, providing a reasonably discernible path for appellate review.

## Kamdem-Ouaffo v. Baker Botts, LLP

- Appeal: 2026-1282
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Order (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Appeal dismissed; pending motions denied; each side to bear its own costs.
- Tags: standing / jurisdiction, waiver / forfeiture, non-precedential, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/26-1282.ORDER.4-30-2026_2685370.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit dismissed the appeal as clearly frivolous without resolving whether jurisdiction lay in the Federal Circuit or the Second Circuit, because appellant failed to make any cogent, non-frivolous argument for relief under Rule 60(b)(4)'s exacting 'void judgment' standard. Transfer to the Second Circuit under 28 U.S.C. § 1631 was declined as not in the interest of justice.

**Why it matters:** Illustrates that the Federal Circuit will dismiss a frivolous appeal outright—without deciding its own jurisdiction—rather than transfer it, when no non-frivolous argument for relief exists regardless of which circuit has jurisdiction.

**Key points:**
- Appellant's underlying inventorship correction claim against PepsiCo was dismissed by the SDNY and affirmed by the Federal Circuit in 2016; a subsequent fraud-on-the-court action was dismissed and affirmed by the Second Circuit.
- Appellant's Rule 60(b)(4) motion argued the prior dismissal was jurisdictionally defective because his state claims raised a substantial federal question; the district court denied it as an attempt to relitigate the prior dismissal.
- The Federal Circuit expressly declined to resolve the jurisdictional question (Federal Circuit vs. Second Circuit under § 1295(a)(1)), invoking its authority to dismiss regardless of how that question would be answered.
- Rule 60(b)(4) relief requires showing the judgment is void—i.e., the court lacked jurisdiction or violated due process—a standard appellant failed to meet with any cogent, non-frivolous argument.
- Because the appeal was clearly frivolous, the court declined to transfer to the Second Circuit even assuming that court had jurisdiction, finding transfer not in the interest of justice under 28 U.S.C. § 1631.
