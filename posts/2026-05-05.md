# Federal Circuit Patent Opinions - 2026-05-05

## Janssen Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Tolmar, Inc.

- Appeal: 2024-1699, 2024-1700, 2024-1980, 2024-2020
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Order (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Remanded; mandate issued May 5, 2026.
- Tags: remand, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA, non-precedential, standing / jurisdiction
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1699.ORDER.5-5-2026_2688218.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit granted the joint motion to remand so the district court can act on its indicative ruling, but expressly took no position on whether vacatur of the final judgment is appropriate under Bancorp. Each side bears its own costs.

**Why it matters:** Counsel handling settled appeals should note that the Federal Circuit will facilitate a remand for an indicative-ruling consent judgment but will leave the Bancorp vacatur analysis entirely to the district court—meaning vacatur of the underlying merits judgment is not guaranteed even after settlement.

**Key points:**
- Parties settled all claims and jointly moved to remand in light of the district court's April 3, 2026 indicative ruling under Fed. R. Civ. P. 62.1 that it would vacate the March 13, 2024 final judgment and enter a consent judgment if remanded.
- The Federal Circuit granted the remand but explicitly reserved the Bancorp question, directing the district court to apply United States Bancorp Mortgage Co. v. Bonner Mall Partnership, 513 U.S. 18 (1994), when deciding whether to vacate its prior judgment.
- Under Bancorp, vacatur on mootness caused by settlement is disfavored absent exceptional circumstances—the district court retains discretion to deny vacatur even with a consent judgment in hand.
- The order is nonprecedential and resolves only the procedural remand; no merits issues were decided by the Federal Circuit.
- Each side bears its own costs, consistent with a negotiated resolution.

## Nike, Inc. v. Lululemon USA Inc.

- Appeal: 2026-1275
- Origin: PTO
- Document: Order (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Dismissed with prejudice by stipulation; each party bears its own costs; mandate issued May 5, 2026.
- Tags: non-precedential, standing / jurisdiction, waiver / forfeiture, remand, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/26-1275.ORDER.5-5-2026_2688630.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit dismissed the appeal by stipulation of the parties under Fed. R. App. P. 42(b). Each side bears its own costs.

**Why it matters:** Signals a likely settlement or license between Nike and Lululemon resolving the IPR challenge before the Federal Circuit reached the merits.

**Key points:**
- Nike appealed a PTAB decision in IPR2024-00460 challenging a Lululemon patent; the underlying technology area is not specified in the extracted text.
- Both parties jointly stipulated to dismissal under Fed. R. App. P. 42(b), indicating a voluntary resolution—likely a settlement or licensing agreement.
- The Federal Circuit granted the stipulated dismissal and ordered each side to bear its own costs, with no fee-shifting.
- The mandate issued the same day as the order (May 5, 2026), making the dismissal immediately final.
- The order is nonprecedential and has no substantive legal holding on the merits of the IPR.

## TJTM Technologies, LLC v. Google LLC

- Appeal: 25-1218
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: § 101, claim construction, infringement, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/25-1218.OPINION.5-5-2026_2688177.pdf

**Holding:** Representative claim 1 of U.S. Patent No. 8,958,853 is directed to the abstract idea of 'screening notifications' and recites only generic, well-known components arranged in a conventional manner, failing both steps of the Alice framework. The Federal Circuit affirmed dismissal under § 101.

**Why it matters:** Confirms that adding a user-benefit feature (auto-suppressing phone notifications when paired with a vehicle) to generic mobile hardware does not constitute a technological improvement sufficient to survive § 101 at the pleading stage, and that conclusory inventive-concept allegations cannot defeat a Rule 12(b)(6) motion.

**Key points:**
- The '853 patent claims a mobile device that automatically enters 'inactive mode'—suppressing notifications and sending an away message—when paired with a vehicle; the district court and Federal Circuit both characterized this as the abstract idea of 'screening notifications.'
- Under Alice step one, the court rejected TJTM's analogy to Contour IP and Enfish, holding that improving user experience through a different operational mode does not amount to a technological improvement to the underlying mobile phone technology.
- Under Alice step two, the court found all claim components (wireless communication module, processor, memory, GUI, away message) generic and well-known, combined in conventional fashion; TJTM's amended complaint offered only conclusory assertions of an inventive concept.
- At oral argument, TJTM effectively conceded that Bluetooth-style device pairing was known in the field and was not itself an inventive aspect of the claims, undermining its primary step-two argument.
- This is a nonprecedential opinion, but it reinforces existing Federal Circuit doctrine—drawn from Trinity Info Media and Cellspin—that conclusory pleading allegations cannot rescue an abstract-idea claim from § 101 dismissal.
