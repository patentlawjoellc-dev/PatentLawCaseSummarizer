# Federal Circuit Patent Opinions - 2026-05-06

## DK Crown Holdings Inc. v. AG 18, LLC

- Appeal: 24-2078
- Origin: PTO
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: § 103, § 102, waiver / forfeiture, claim preclusion, remand, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-2078.OPINION.5-6-2026_2689273.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed, holding that the Board did not abuse its discretion in refusing to consider DK's reply-footnote request to apply Ground 3 (Bryson + Schlottmann) against claim 18 when the petition never asserted Schlottmann against that claim. The court also rejected DK's collateral estoppel argument that invalidation of claims collectively containing all limitations of claim 18 rendered claim 18 invalid.

**Why it matters:** Reinforces that IPR petitioners are strictly bound by the grounds asserted in the petition—post-institution attempts to add new prior art references against a claim, even via footnote, will not be entertained, and collateral estoppel cannot be manufactured by aggregating limitations from separately invalidated claims.

**Key points:**
- DK's IPR petition challenged claim 18 only under Grounds 1 and 2 (Bryson alone); Ground 3 (Bryson + Schlottmann) was directed solely at claims 15–17 in the petition's own summary scorecard.
- After institution, DK sought in a reply footnote to have the Board analyze claim 18 under Ground 3 'instead of Grounds 1/2'—the Board refused, finding this exceeded proper reply scope by adding a new prior art reference to fill a gap in the petition.
- The Federal Circuit found no abuse of discretion: the petition contained no cross-reference or indication that Ground 3 or Schlottmann was intended to apply to claim 18, distinguishing Voice Tech Corp. v. Unified Patents (typographical error with substantive cross-reference) on its facts.
- DK's collateral estoppel theory—that invalidation of claims 10, 12, 15, 16, and 17 collectively covering all limitations of claim 18 estops AG 18 from defending claim 18—was rejected; the Federal Circuit has never applied collateral estoppel by 'cobbling together limitations from disparate claims' to replicate another claim.
- Opinion is nonprecedential but consistent with and citing precedential authority (Netflix, Inc. v. DivX, LLC; Yita LLC v. MacNeil IP LLC) on the strict petition-scope rule.

## Nike, Inc. v. Lululemon USA Inc.

- Appeal: 25-1709
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Order (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Dismissed; mandate issued May 6, 2026.
- Tags: non-precedential, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/25-1709.ORDER.5-6-2026_2689578.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit dismissed the appeal by stipulation of the parties under Fed. R. App. P. 42(b), with each side bearing its own costs.

**Why it matters:** This is a routine stipulated dismissal with no substantive ruling; it signals the parties likely reached a settlement, but the underlying merits and any district court outcome remain unreviewed by the Federal Circuit.

**Key points:**
- Appeal arose from S.D.N.Y. Case No. 1:23-cv-00771-AS, Nike v. Lululemon.
- Both parties agreed to dismissal under Fed. R. App. P. 42(b); no contested merits ruling was issued.
- Each side bears its own appellate costs — a common feature of negotiated resolutions.
- Order is nonprecedential and has no doctrinal significance.
- Mandate issued the same day as the order, May 6, 2026, closing the Federal Circuit proceeding.

## Starlight Entertainment Enterprises, Inc. v. Central District of California

- Appeal: 2026-1609
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Order (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Appeal dismissed; all other motions denied; show-cause order issued to appellant within 21 days regarding potential sanctions including monetary fine and/or anti-filing injunction.
- Tags: standing / jurisdiction, attorney fees / sanctions, non-precedential, injunction / stay
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/26-1609.ORDER.5-6-2026_2689301.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit dismissed the appeal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction and untimeliness, and issued an order to show cause why sanctions—including an anti-filing injunction—should not be imposed, given Calderon Lopez's repeated disregard of prior warnings.

**Why it matters:** Illustrates the Federal Circuit's willingness to move toward affirmative filing restrictions against serial vexatious appellants who repeatedly ignore jurisdictional warnings, a tool patent counsel should be aware of when dealing with abusive filers.

**Key points:**
- The Federal Circuit has no jurisdiction over Ninth Circuit decisions and has told Calderon Lopez so repeatedly across more than a dozen prior appeals.
- The appeal was also independently defective as untimely, filed well outside the 30-day jurisdictional deadline from any cognizable district court order.
- The underlying dispute—a filing-fee refund request arising from a landlord-tenant case—falls entirely outside the Federal Circuit's limited subject matter jurisdiction.
- The court had previously warned Calderon Lopez in September 2025 (Appeal No. 2025-1943) that further improper filings could result in sanctions; he filed this appeal anyway.
- Potential sanctions identified include a monetary fine, a payment-conditioned filing restriction, and/or an anti-filing injunction requiring pre-approval of any future notices of appeal to this court.

## Lu v. Hyper Bicycles, Inc.

- Appeal: 25-1110
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: attorney fees / sanctions, non-precedential, infringement, waiver / forfeiture
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/25-1110.OPINION.5-6-2026_2689312.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed the district court's § 285 exceptional-case finding and attorney fee award, holding that the district court did not abuse its discretion where the record showed plaintiff failed to conduct any meaningful discovery, submitted cursory summary judgment briefing, violated local rules, and possessed no evidence of infringing sales from at least January 3, 2022 onward.

**Why it matters:** Reinforces that a plaintiff's failure to conduct discovery, absence of evidence of infringement, and dilatory litigation conduct can independently support an exceptional-case finding and a fee award cut off from the date counsel should have known the claims were meritless.

**Key points:**
- Plaintiff filed a patent infringement suit in 2020, lost on summary judgment, and had that loss affirmed; defendant then moved for § 285 fees.
- District court found the case exceptional based on bad-faith prosecution and unnecessary cost inflation: plaintiff sought four discovery extensions but never propounded written discovery, took no depositions, and designated no experts.
- Plaintiff's own discovery responses admitted he had no knowledge of actual infringing sales and no records identifying infringing products from at least January 3, 2022—the date the court used as the fee-award cutoff.
- Federal Circuit reviewed all aspects of the § 285 determination for abuse of discretion and found none, deferring to the district court's prolonged familiarity with the case under Highmark.
- Arguments raised for the first time in plaintiff's reply brief were not considered, consistent with Federal Circuit practice.
