# Federal Circuit Patent Opinions - 2026-05-19

## Recor Medical, Inc. v. Medtronic Ireland Manufacturing Unlimited Co.

- Appeal: 25-1998
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Reversed and remanded. Costs to appellant Medtronic Ireland.
- Tags: standing / jurisdiction, remand, non-precedential, infringement
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/25-1998.OPINION.5-19-2026_2695895.pdf

**Holding:** A patent owner that retains a secondary right to sue, royalty interests, and veto authority over sublicenses under an exclusive license agreement retains a non-illusory exclusionary right sufficient to satisfy the Article III constitutional minimum for standing. Because Medtronic Ireland's retained rights were materially identical to those found sufficient in the companion precedential decision A.L.M. Holding Co. v. Zydex Industries Private Ltd. (No. 25-1317), the district court's dismissal for lack of constitutional standing was reversed.

**Why it matters:** Patent owners who have granted exclusive licenses but retained a secondary right to sue, sublicense veto rights, and royalty interests should have constitutional standing to assert their patents directly—even without joining the exclusive licensee—so long as those retained rights are not rendered illusory by the licensee's granted rights.

**Key points:**
- Medtronic Ireland, as patent owner of the '629 and '085 renal neuromodulation patents, granted Medtronic Galway an exclusive license to make, use, and sell designated 'Products,' but retained: (1) a secondary right to sue for infringement if Galway declines to act; (2) royalty interests including on sublicensee sales; and (3) veto authority over any sublicenses.
- The district court relied on Morrow v. Microsoft Corp. to hold that a contractual right to sue, standing alone and separated from all other patent rights, does not confer constitutional standing—but the Federal Circuit distinguished Morrow because Medtronic Ireland's right to sue was not illusory: Galway could not unilaterally grant a royalty-free sublicense to an accused infringer without Medtronic Ireland's consent.
- The court applied its same-day precedential ruling in A.L.M. Holding Co. v. Zydex Industries Private Ltd. (No. 25-1317), holding that a patent owner's retained right to sue is not illusory where the licensee cannot nullify it through unilateral, royalty-free sublicensing—and that this principle controls here because Medtronic Ireland's retained rights are materially the same.
- The court also noted that the exclusive license grant is limited to 'Products' specifically designated by Medtronic Ireland, meaning Medtronic Ireland retains full exclusionary rights over any non-designated finished devices and all unfinished medical products.
- The appeal was not moot despite Medtronic's amendment of the ELA and filing of a new lawsuit (Recor II), because reversal would have concrete legal consequences—including undermining Recor's unclean hands defense in Recor II, which depends on the premise that Medtronic Ireland lacked sufficient rights to sue in Recor I.

## A.L.M. Holding Company v. Zydex Industries Private Ltd.

- Appeal: 25-1317
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Opinion (Precedential)
- Disposition: Reversed and remanded; costs to appellant.
- Tags: standing / jurisdiction, infringement, remand, precedential, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/25-1317.OPINION.5-19-2026_2695934.pdf

**Holding:** A patent owner that retains a right to sue, a sublicensing veto (with pass-through royalties), and royalty interests retains a non-illusory exclusionary right sufficient for Article III constitutional standing, even after granting an exclusive license to commercialize the patents. The district court applied too demanding a threshold by treating the retained right to sue as insufficient standing alone, without accounting for the combined effect of the sublicensing veto and royalty interests that prevented the licensee from unilaterally extinguishing the owner's enforcement right.

**Why it matters:** Patent owners who retain a right to sue paired with sublicensing veto rights and royalty interests have Article III standing even under a broad exclusive license—and statutory standing cases like Mann remain instructive on the constitutional standing inquiry, contrary to the approach some district courts had taken of treating the two analyses as entirely separate.

**Key points:**
- Patent owner A.L.M./Ergon licensed exclusive commercialization rights to Ingevity but retained: (1) the right to sue third-party infringers (with shared or sole control depending on whether Ingevity elects to join), (2) a veto over Ingevity's sublicenses (not to be unreasonably withheld, but requiring consent and pass-through royalties), and (3) royalty interests including from sublicensees.
- The Federal Circuit held these retained rights collectively constitute a non-illusory exclusionary right for Article III purposes because Ingevity cannot unilaterally grant a royalty-free sublicense to an accused infringer and thereby moot the patent owner's suit—the key distinction from Morrow, where the plaintiff held only a bare contractual right to sue stripped of all other patent rights.
- The court clarified that statutory standing cases (particularly Mann) are instructive—though not controlling—on the constitutional standing inquiry: the factual question of whether a licensee's powers render the patent owner's enforcement right illusory arises under both analyses, and Zebra Technologies had already cited Mann approvingly in a constitutional standing context.
- The court rejected the district court's bright-line separation of constitutional and statutory standing precedents, holding that the same facts bearing on 'all substantial rights' can also bear on whether the owner retained a concrete exclusionary interest, though the two legal standards remain distinct.
- Morrow distinguished: Morrow involved a non-owner licensee holding only a bare right to sue, where the patent owner could moot any suit by granting a royalty-free sublicense; here, the plaintiff is the patent owner and Ingevity's sublicensing right is constrained by consent and royalty obligations that preserve the owner's enforcement interest.
