# Federal Circuit Patent Opinions - 2026-05-22

## Versata Software, LLC v. Ford Motor Company

- Appeal: 2024-1140, 2024-1206, 2024-1234
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Opinion (Precedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed-in-part (trade secret liability), reversed-in-part (breach-of-contract damages reinstated at $82.26M), vacated-in-part and remanded (trade secret damages for new trial with instruction to reconsider previously excluded unjust enrichment and value-based royalty models).
- Tags: damages, remand, precedential, standing / jurisdiction
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1140.OPINION.5-22-2026_2698249.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit held that the district court legally erred by categorically precluding Versata from seeking unjust enrichment damages under the DTSA and MUTSA, which expressly authorize such recovery, and vacated the trade secret damages judgment for a new trial; the court also reversed the JMOL reduction of the breach-of-contract jury award from $82.26M to $3, reinstating the full verdict as supported by sufficient evidence. The court affirmed denial of Ford's JMOL on trade secret liability, holding that neither the DTSA nor MUTSA requires a plaintiff to prove a defendant's specific knowledge of each element of a combination trade secret.

**Why it matters:** Counsel litigating trade secret misappropriation under the DTSA or state uniform trade secrets acts should note that unjust enrichment damages are a statutory right that cannot be categorically foreclosed by a court simply because the parties have a licensing history, and that combination trade secret claims do not require proof of a defendant's granular knowledge of each component of the combination.

**Key points:**
- The DTSA and MUTSA expressly permit unjust enrichment damages as an alternative or supplement to actual loss; the district court abused its discretion by confining Versata solely to a reasonable-royalty model tied to the parties' licensing history, which the Federal Circuit found was not compelled by Sixth Circuit precedent (Vitro Corp. or Mid-Michigan).
- On trade secret liability, neither the DTSA nor MUTSA imposes a heightened knowledge requirement that a defendant specifically knew the precise combination elements of a combination trade secret—consistent with the Sixth Circuit's rejection of a similar argument in Caudill Seed.
- The $82.26M breach-of-contract jury award was reinstated because Versata presented three licensing-history-based damages figures, expert testimony distinguishing base license fees from support fees, and the MSSA and its 2011 addendum—sufficient for the jury to calculate damages with reasonable (not mathematical) certainty under Michigan law.
- The Federal Circuit vacated the trade secret damages judgment and remanded for a new trial, instructing the district court to reconsider the two previously excluded reasonable-royalty models that incorporated value-to-Ford components beyond the parties' licensing history.
- Federal Circuit jurisdiction was proper notwithstanding dismissal of the patent claims, because the appeal arose from a final judgment in a case that originated with patent claims under 28 U.S.C. § 1295(a)(1).

## Pictometry International Corporation v. Roofr Inc.

- Appeal: 24-2321
- Origin: PTO
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: § 103, claim construction, remand, non-precedential, waiver / forfeiture
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-2321.OPINION.5-22-2026_2698213.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed the PTAB's finding that all claims of the '538 patent are unpatentable for obviousness, holding that substantial evidence supported the Board's conclusion that Bailey's 'predominant slope' discloses the claimed 'predominant pitch,' and that using a weighted average to calculate predominant pitch would have been the application of a known technique yielding a predictable result.

**Why it matters:** Confirms that a patentee's failure to squarely present a claim-construction position before the PTAB after institution can doom a claim-construction argument on appeal, and that the Board may rely on a skilled artisan's background knowledge—including known mathematical techniques like weighted averages—to supply an obviousness gap even when the prior art does not expressly disclose the limitation.

**Key points:**
- U.S. Patent No. 9,183,538 claims a computer-implemented method for estimating roof area from aerial imagery using a roof's 'footprint' and 'predominant pitch'; Roofr petitioned for IPR on obviousness grounds relying primarily on the Bailey (Army Corps of Engineers) article and the Pershing patent application.
- Pictometry failed to propose an express claim construction for 'predominant pitch' in its post-institution papers; its attempt to raise a claim-construction argument at oral argument before the Board—and again on appeal—was therefore at risk of waiver, though the Federal Circuit resolved the issue on the merits regardless.
- The court held that 'predominant pitch' is not facially limited to a weighted average of all roof sections; Bailey's 'predominant slope' falls within the ordinary meaning of the term, and the Board's implied construction was supported by substantial evidence including expert testimony and a construction-industry dictionary.
- For dependent claims 11 and 18 (which require a weighted average), the Board permissibly found it would have been obvious to use a weighted average—a known technique—to calculate predominant pitch, consistent with KSR's teaching that courts may account for the inferences and creative steps a skilled artisan would employ.
- The Board's citation to Bailey's slope-factor chart in the claim 18 analysis was not erroneous because the Board relied on a combination of references, not Bailey alone, for that determination.

## Pictometry International Corporation v. Roofr Inc.

- Appeal: 24-2322
- Origin: PTO
- Document: Opinion (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Affirmed
- Tags: § 103, claim construction, remand, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-2322.OPINION.5-22-2026_2698225.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's finding that all claims of the '800 patent are unpatentable for obviousness, holding that substantial evidence supported the Board's determination that a skilled artisan would have known to sequence Abhyanker's marker-locking feature after marker placement, satisfying the claimed 'user-acceptance' limitation even under Pictometry's narrower temporal interpretation.

**Why it matters:** Illustrates that a patent owner's proposed temporal claim construction will not save claims from obviousness where the prior art, with ordinary creativity, can be modified to satisfy the sequence—and where the patent's own figures undercut the proposed narrow construction.

**Key points:**
- The '800 patent claims a roof-measurement process in which a user moves a marker on a satellite image to identify a roof location and then provides 'user-acceptance' (an affirmative confirmation step) of that location.
- Pictometry argued Abhyanker's marker-locking feature did not satisfy the 'user-acceptance' limitation because the lock option was available before—not responsive to—the user's marker designation; the Board rejected this temporal argument and found the claims obvious even assuming Pictometry's narrower reading.
- The Federal Circuit declined to resolve the claim-construction dispute, noting that Pictometry's own patent figures (Figs. 4A–4D) show the 'confirm selection' button present before the user enters an address, creating a significant obstacle to the proposed narrow construction under the principle that fundamental embodiments should not be read out of claims.
- The court found no reversible error in the Board's conclusion that a skilled artisan with ordinary creativity would sequence Abhyanker's lock-pushpin option to operate after marker placement, satisfying the temporal requirement even under Pictometry's interpretation.
- Claim 17 was disposed of summarily because Pictometry offered no independent argument distinguishing it from claims 1 and 11.

## Arsenis v. United States Trustee

- Appeal: 26-1685
- Origin: DCT
- Document: Order (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Granted motion to transfer; entire matter, including all pending motions, transferred to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit.
- Tags: standing / jurisdiction, remand, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/26-1685.ORDER.5-22-2026_2698274.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit lacks jurisdiction over appeals originating from bankruptcy court decisions and transferred the case to the Third Circuit under 28 U.S.C. § 1631. Arsenis failed to demonstrate any basis for Federal Circuit jurisdiction.

**Why it matters:** A straightforward reminder that the Federal Circuit's jurisdiction does not extend to bankruptcy appeals, which follow the regional circuit path under 28 U.S.C. §§ 158 and 1295.

**Key points:**
- Arsenis appealed a D.N.J. district court mootness dismissal of her bankruptcy appeal to the Federal Circuit rather than the Third Circuit.
- The Federal Circuit's jurisdiction is limited and does not generally encompass appeals from bankruptcy court decisions; the proper appellate path runs through the regional circuit under 28 U.S.C. §§ 158(a) and 158(d).
- Arsenis offered no basis to bring the case within the Federal Circuit's limited jurisdiction, and the court cited In re Cambridge Biotech Corp. as illustrative of the narrow exceptions.
- Pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1631, the court transferred rather than dismissed, finding transfer in the interest of justice.
- All pending motions, including Arsenis's emergency stay motion, were transferred along with the merits and will be resolved by the Third Circuit.
