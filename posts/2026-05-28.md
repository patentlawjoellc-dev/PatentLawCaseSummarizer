# Federal Circuit Patent Opinions - 2026-05-28

## Insulet Corp. v. EOFlow, Co. Ltd.

- Appeal: 25-1807
- Origin: DCT
- Document: OPINION (Precedential)
- Disposition: Reversed the district court's judgment; costs awarded to EOFlow.
- Tags: standing / jurisdiction, infringement, damages, injunction / stay, precedential, Hatch-Waxman / ANDA
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/25-1807.OPINION.5-28-2026_2700697.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit held that it had appellate jurisdiction because the nominally without-prejudice dismissal of Insulet's patent claims functioned at least in part as a dismissal with prejudice once limitations had run on alleged 2018 infringement. On the merits, the court held EOFlow was entitled to JMOL because Insulet knew or should have known facts sufficient to plead DTSA misappropriation more than three years before filing suit.

**Why it matters:** The decision is a precedential reminder that dismissed patent claims can preserve Federal Circuit jurisdiction when time-barred acts make the dismissal functionally with prejudice, and it applies a rigorous accrual analysis to trade-secret claims tied to access-plus-similarity evidence.

**Key points:**
- Jurisdiction: although the patent claims were dismissed "without prejudice," alleged June 2018 infringement was time-barred by the time of dismissal, so the dismissal altered the parties' legal positions and supported Federal Circuit jurisdiction.
- DTSA limitations: the court did not choose between inquiry notice and the Merck discovery standard, holding that Insulet's claim was untimely even under the more demanding discovery-rule formulation.
- Pleading/accrual framework: the majority held that knowledge of a former employee's access to trade secrets plus similarity between the accused product and the asserted trade secrets can be sufficient to plead misappropriation and start the limitations clock.
- Single-claim treatment: because the alleged disclosures were part of the same course of conduct by the same former employee for the same purpose, the court treated the asserted trade secrets as subject to the same limitations analysis.
- Dissent: Judge Prost agreed on jurisdiction but would have upheld the jury's limitations findings, criticizing the majority for effectively applying inquiry notice and reweighing trial evidence.

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit dismissed the appeals by agreement of the parties under Rule 42(b). Each side will bear its own costs.

**Why it matters:** This nonprecedential order is a procedural termination only and provides no substantive patent-law guidance.

**Key points:**
- The order resolves Federal Circuit appeal Nos. 2025-1628 and 2025-1629 from the Eastern District of Texas.

- Each side bears its own costs, and the order issued as the mandate on the same date.

## GO1 PTY, LTD. v. OPENSESAME, INC.

- Appeal: 24-1762
- Origin: PTO
- Document: OPINION (Nonprecedential)
- Disposition: Vacated and remanded; costs awarded to appellant Go1.
- Tags: § 103, remand, non-precedential
- Source: https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/24-1762.OPINION.5-28-2026_2700679.pdf

**Holding:** The Federal Circuit held that substantial evidence did not support the Board's finding that Go1's expert testimony on motivation to modify was conclusory. The court vacated and remanded for the Board to reconsider obviousness consistent with the opinion.

**Why it matters:** The decision reinforces that, for simple and predictable sequencing changes, expert testimony grounded in a POSA's common sense and ordinary creativity may be enough without a separate prior-art reference expressly teaching the exact sequence.

**Key points:**
- The claim required the licensing/reporting server to verify license validity and, upon verification, provide a location designator for accessing the content player.
- Madison disclosed the reverse order—providing a redirector file before access verification—and the Board found that gap fatal to Go1's obviousness challenge.
- Go1's expert testified that the sequencing difference was insubstantial and would have been an obvious design choice to a skilled artisan combining Madison with Sperle.
- The Federal Circuit held the Board applied too rigid an approach under KSR by discounting ordinary creativity, common sense, and the finite, predictable nature of the two sequencing options.
- The court did not hold the claims unpatentable; it remanded for further PTAB proceedings.
