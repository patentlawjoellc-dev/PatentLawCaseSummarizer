Versata Software, LLC v. Ford Motor Company

Holding: The Federal Circuit held that the district court legally erred by categorically precluding Versata from seeking unjust enrichment damages under the DTSA and MUTSA, which expressly authorize such recovery, and vacated the trade secret damages judgment for a new trial; the court also reversed the JMOL reduction of the breach-of-contract jury award from $82.26M to $3, reinstating the full verdict as supported by sufficient evidence. The court affirmed denial of Ford's JMOL on trade secret liability, holding that neither the DTSA nor MUTSA requires a plaintiff to prove a defendant's specific knowledge of each element of a combination trade secret.

Disposition: Affirmed-in-part (trade secret liability), reversed-in-part (breach-of-contract damages reinstated at $82.26M), vacated-in-part and remanded (trade secret damages for new trial with instruction to reconsider previously excluded unjust enrichment and value-based royalty models).

Why it matters: Counsel litigating trade secret misappropriation under the DTSA or state uniform trade secrets acts should note that unjust enrichment damages are a statutory right that cannot be categorically foreclosed by a court simply because the parties have a licensing history, and that combination trade secret claims do not require proof of a defendant's granular knowledge of each component of the combination.

damagesremandprecedentialstanding / jurisdiction
  • The DTSA and MUTSA expressly permit unjust enrichment damages as an alternative or supplement to actual loss; the district court abused its discretion by confining Versata solely to a reasonable-royalty model tied to the parties' licensing history, which the Federal Circuit found was not compelled by Sixth Circuit precedent (Vitro Corp. or Mid-Michigan).
  • On trade secret liability, neither the DTSA nor MUTSA imposes a heightened knowledge requirement that a defendant specifically knew the precise combination elements of a combination trade secret—consistent with the Sixth Circuit's rejection of a similar argument in Caudill Seed.
  • The $82.26M breach-of-contract jury award was reinstated because Versata presented three licensing-history-based damages figures, expert testimony distinguishing base license fees from support fees, and the MSSA and its 2011 addendum—sufficient for the jury to calculate damages with reasonable (not mathematical) certainty under Michigan law.
  • The Federal Circuit vacated the trade secret damages judgment and remanded for a new trial, instructing the district court to reconsider the two previously excluded reasonable-royalty models that incorporated value-to-Ford components beyond the parties' licensing history.
  • Federal Circuit jurisdiction was proper notwithstanding dismissal of the patent claims, because the appeal arose from a final judgment in a case that originated with patent claims under 28 U.S.C. § 1295(a)(1).

PTOOpinionNonprecedential24-2321

Pictometry International Corporation v. Roofr Inc.

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed the PTAB's finding that all claims of the '538 patent are unpatentable for obviousness, holding that substantial evidence supported the Board's conclusion that Bailey's 'predominant slope' discloses the claimed 'predominant pitch,' and that using a weighted average to calculate predominant pitch would have been the application of a known technique yielding a predictable result.

Disposition: Affirmed

Why it matters: Confirms that a patentee's failure to squarely present a claim-construction position before the PTAB after institution can doom a claim-construction argument on appeal, and that the Board may rely on a skilled artisan's background knowledge—including known mathematical techniques like weighted averages—to supply an obviousness gap even when the prior art does not expressly disclose the limitation.

§ 103claim constructionremandnon-precedentialwaiver / forfeiture
  • U.S. Patent No. 9,183,538 claims a computer-implemented method for estimating roof area from aerial imagery using a roof's 'footprint' and 'predominant pitch'; Roofr petitioned for IPR on obviousness grounds relying primarily on the Bailey (Army Corps of Engineers) article and the Pershing patent application.
  • Pictometry failed to propose an express claim construction for 'predominant pitch' in its post-institution papers; its attempt to raise a claim-construction argument at oral argument before the Board—and again on appeal—was therefore at risk of waiver, though the Federal Circuit resolved the issue on the merits regardless.
  • The court held that 'predominant pitch' is not facially limited to a weighted average of all roof sections; Bailey's 'predominant slope' falls within the ordinary meaning of the term, and the Board's implied construction was supported by substantial evidence including expert testimony and a construction-industry dictionary.
  • For dependent claims 11 and 18 (which require a weighted average), the Board permissibly found it would have been obvious to use a weighted average—a known technique—to calculate predominant pitch, consistent with KSR's teaching that courts may account for the inferences and creative steps a skilled artisan would employ.
  • The Board's citation to Bailey's slope-factor chart in the claim 18 analysis was not erroneous because the Board relied on a combination of references, not Bailey alone, for that determination.

PTOOpinionNonprecedential24-2322

Pictometry International Corporation v. Roofr Inc.

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's finding that all claims of the '800 patent are unpatentable for obviousness, holding that substantial evidence supported the Board's determination that a skilled artisan would have known to sequence Abhyanker's marker-locking feature after marker placement, satisfying the claimed 'user-acceptance' limitation even under Pictometry's narrower temporal interpretation.

Disposition: Affirmed

Why it matters: Illustrates that a patent owner's proposed temporal claim construction will not save claims from obviousness where the prior art, with ordinary creativity, can be modified to satisfy the sequence—and where the patent's own figures undercut the proposed narrow construction.

§ 103claim constructionremandnon-precedential
  • The '800 patent claims a roof-measurement process in which a user moves a marker on a satellite image to identify a roof location and then provides 'user-acceptance' (an affirmative confirmation step) of that location.
  • Pictometry argued Abhyanker's marker-locking feature did not satisfy the 'user-acceptance' limitation because the lock option was available before—not responsive to—the user's marker designation; the Board rejected this temporal argument and found the claims obvious even assuming Pictometry's narrower reading.
  • The Federal Circuit declined to resolve the claim-construction dispute, noting that Pictometry's own patent figures (Figs. 4A–4D) show the 'confirm selection' button present before the user enters an address, creating a significant obstacle to the proposed narrow construction under the principle that fundamental embodiments should not be read out of claims.
  • The court found no reversible error in the Board's conclusion that a skilled artisan with ordinary creativity would sequence Abhyanker's lock-pushpin option to operate after marker placement, satisfying the temporal requirement even under Pictometry's interpretation.
  • Claim 17 was disposed of summarily because Pictometry offered no independent argument distinguishing it from claims 1 and 11.

Arsenis v. United States Trustee

Holding: The Federal Circuit lacks jurisdiction over appeals originating from bankruptcy court decisions and transferred the case to the Third Circuit under 28 U.S.C. § 1631. Arsenis failed to demonstrate any basis for Federal Circuit jurisdiction.

Disposition: Granted motion to transfer; entire matter, including all pending motions, transferred to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit.

Why it matters: A straightforward reminder that the Federal Circuit's jurisdiction does not extend to bankruptcy appeals, which follow the regional circuit path under 28 U.S.C. §§ 158 and 1295.

standing / jurisdictionremandnon-precedential
  • Arsenis appealed a D.N.J. district court mootness dismissal of her bankruptcy appeal to the Federal Circuit rather than the Third Circuit.
  • The Federal Circuit's jurisdiction is limited and does not generally encompass appeals from bankruptcy court decisions; the proper appellate path runs through the regional circuit under 28 U.S.C. §§ 158(a) and 158(d).
  • Arsenis offered no basis to bring the case within the Federal Circuit's limited jurisdiction, and the court cited In re Cambridge Biotech Corp. as illustrative of the narrow exceptions.
  • Pursuant to 28 U.S.C. § 1631, the court transferred rather than dismissed, finding transfer in the interest of justice.
  • All pending motions, including Arsenis's emergency stay motion, were transferred along with the merits and will be resolved by the Third Circuit.

DCTOpinionNonprecedential24-2297

Otsuka Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd. v. Lupin Ltd.

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed non-infringement of both patents because Otsuka failed to prove by a preponderance that Lupin's DMF process reaches practical completion of the reduction reaction before more than one molar equivalent of sodium borohydride is added. The court also affirmed invalidity of the asserted '735 patent claims for obviousness, finding no clear error in the district court's motivation-to-combine and secondary-considerations analyses, and upheld forfeiture of Otsuka's challenge to Lupin's expert's qualifications.

Disposition: Affirmed

Why it matters: In Hatch-Waxman cases involving process and product-by-process claims with quantitative reagent limitations, patentees bear the evidentiary burden of proving that the accused ANDA process actually operates within the claimed range — general expert experience and flawed experimental data will not carry that burden at the clear-error standard.

§ 103infringementclaim constructionHatch-Waxman / ANDAwaiver / forfeiturenon-precedential
  • Non-infringement (both patents): The asserted claims require a hydrogenating agent in an amount of 0.25–1 (or 0.25–0.5) molar equivalents; Lupin's DMF process adds at least 1.2 molar equivalents of sodium borohydride. The district court construed 'amount' as the quantity present while the reaction is 'taking place' until practical completion — a construction Otsuka itself advocated. Even accepting that construction, Otsuka's only evidence (Experiments 109/115 and Dr. Roush's expert testimony) was undermined by data anomalies, different reaction conditions, and reliance on the same flawed experiments, so the Federal Circuit found no clear error in the non-infringement finding.
  • Forfeiture of expert-qualification challenge: Otsuka failed to file a Daubert motion or timely object at trial to Lupin's expert Dr. Dichtel testifying as a person of ordinary skill in the art. Under Third Circuit law, that failure forfeited the challenge; the Federal Circuit reviewed for abuse of discretion and found none, declining to reach the merits.
  • Obviousness of '735 patent claims: The district court found clear and convincing evidence that a POSA would have started with Kondo (a known tolvaptan synthesis reference), been motivated to reduce the sodium borohydride amount in Kondo's reduction step for cost, safety, and process-simplification reasons, and had a reasonable expectation of success. The Federal Circuit found no clear error in crediting Dr. Dichtel's testimony over Dr. Roush's on motivation.
  • Secondary considerations: The Yukawa declaration compared the claimed process to a process using twice Kondo's reagent amount rather than Kondo itself, undermining the unexpected-purity argument. Although the district court apparently overlooked evidence of unexpected yield improvement (30% vs. 82–93%), the Federal Circuit held any error harmless because weak secondary considerations cannot overcome a strong prima facie obviousness case.
  • This is a nonprecedential disposition; it should not be cited as binding authority, though it illustrates the Federal Circuit's application of the clear-error standard to bench-trial infringement findings in quantitative-limitation cases and the importance of timely Daubert practice.

SameSurf, Inc. v. Intuit Inc.

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board's construction of 'web browsing interaction data' as 'data related to web browsing interactions'—broad enough to encompass partially filled webpages—and therefore affirmed the Board's finding that all claims of the '145 patent are unpatentable for obviousness.

Disposition: Affirmed

Why it matters: Reinforces that absent lexicography or clear disclaimer, broad claim terms receive their full plain-and-ordinary-meaning scope, and a patentee cannot narrow a term post hoc by pointing to a single embodiment in the specification.

claim construction§ 103remandnon-precedential
  • The '145 patent claims a method for a guest device to join a synchronized browsing session; the key disputed term was 'web browsing interaction data,' which Samesurf argued was limited to data describing host-device interactions, excluding partially filled webpages.
  • The Board construed the term broadly as 'data related to web browsing interactions,' encompassing partially filled webpages, which led directly to the conclusion that prior art reference Lebrun disclosed the limitation.
  • The Federal Circuit reviewed claim construction de novo and found no lexicography, disclaimer, or claim language requiring the narrower construction Samesurf urged; the specification's reference to a single embodiment was expressly labeled exemplary.
  • The court rejected Samesurf's 'inoperability' argument, noting the claims require only that an application operate 'based on' the web browsing interaction data to access a website server, and Samesurf offered no evidence that partially filled webpages could not satisfy that requirement.
  • This is a nonprecedential disposition and should be cited accordingly.

DCTOpinionNonprecedential25-1998

Recor Medical, Inc. v. Medtronic Ireland Manufacturing Unlimited Co.

Holding: A patent owner that retains a secondary right to sue, royalty interests, and veto authority over sublicenses under an exclusive license agreement retains a non-illusory exclusionary right sufficient to satisfy the Article III constitutional minimum for standing. Because Medtronic Ireland's retained rights were materially identical to those found sufficient in the companion precedential decision A.L.M. Holding Co. v. Zydex Industries Private Ltd. (No. 25-1317), the district court's dismissal for lack of constitutional standing was reversed.

Disposition: Reversed and remanded. Costs to appellant Medtronic Ireland.

Why it matters: Patent owners who have granted exclusive licenses but retained a secondary right to sue, sublicense veto rights, and royalty interests should have constitutional standing to assert their patents directly—even without joining the exclusive licensee—so long as those retained rights are not rendered illusory by the licensee's granted rights.

standing / jurisdictionremandnon-precedentialinfringement
  • Medtronic Ireland, as patent owner of the '629 and '085 renal neuromodulation patents, granted Medtronic Galway an exclusive license to make, use, and sell designated 'Products,' but retained: (1) a secondary right to sue for infringement if Galway declines to act; (2) royalty interests including on sublicensee sales; and (3) veto authority over any sublicenses.
  • The district court relied on Morrow v. Microsoft Corp. to hold that a contractual right to sue, standing alone and separated from all other patent rights, does not confer constitutional standing—but the Federal Circuit distinguished Morrow because Medtronic Ireland's right to sue was not illusory: Galway could not unilaterally grant a royalty-free sublicense to an accused infringer without Medtronic Ireland's consent.
  • The court applied its same-day precedential ruling in A.L.M. Holding Co. v. Zydex Industries Private Ltd. (No. 25-1317), holding that a patent owner's retained right to sue is not illusory where the licensee cannot nullify it through unilateral, royalty-free sublicensing—and that this principle controls here because Medtronic Ireland's retained rights are materially the same.
  • The court also noted that the exclusive license grant is limited to 'Products' specifically designated by Medtronic Ireland, meaning Medtronic Ireland retains full exclusionary rights over any non-designated finished devices and all unfinished medical products.
  • The appeal was not moot despite Medtronic's amendment of the ELA and filing of a new lawsuit (Recor II), because reversal would have concrete legal consequences—including undermining Recor's unclean hands defense in Recor II, which depends on the premise that Medtronic Ireland lacked sufficient rights to sue in Recor I.

DCTOpinionPrecedential25-1317

A.L.M. Holding Company v. Zydex Industries Private Ltd.

Holding: A patent owner that retains a right to sue, a sublicensing veto (with pass-through royalties), and royalty interests retains a non-illusory exclusionary right sufficient for Article III constitutional standing, even after granting an exclusive license to commercialize the patents. The district court applied too demanding a threshold by treating the retained right to sue as insufficient standing alone, without accounting for the combined effect of the sublicensing veto and royalty interests that prevented the licensee from unilaterally extinguishing the owner's enforcement right.

Disposition: Reversed and remanded; costs to appellant.

Why it matters: Patent owners who retain a right to sue paired with sublicensing veto rights and royalty interests have Article III standing even under a broad exclusive license—and statutory standing cases like Mann remain instructive on the constitutional standing inquiry, contrary to the approach some district courts had taken of treating the two analyses as entirely separate.

standing / jurisdictioninfringementremandprecedentialHatch-Waxman / ANDA
  • Patent owner A.L.M./Ergon licensed exclusive commercialization rights to Ingevity but retained: (1) the right to sue third-party infringers (with shared or sole control depending on whether Ingevity elects to join), (2) a veto over Ingevity's sublicenses (not to be unreasonably withheld, but requiring consent and pass-through royalties), and (3) royalty interests including from sublicensees.
  • The Federal Circuit held these retained rights collectively constitute a non-illusory exclusionary right for Article III purposes because Ingevity cannot unilaterally grant a royalty-free sublicense to an accused infringer and thereby moot the patent owner's suit—the key distinction from Morrow, where the plaintiff held only a bare contractual right to sue stripped of all other patent rights.
  • The court clarified that statutory standing cases (particularly Mann) are instructive—though not controlling—on the constitutional standing inquiry: the factual question of whether a licensee's powers render the patent owner's enforcement right illusory arises under both analyses, and Zebra Technologies had already cited Mann approvingly in a constitutional standing context.
  • The court rejected the district court's bright-line separation of constitutional and statutory standing precedents, holding that the same facts bearing on 'all substantial rights' can also bear on whether the owner retained a concrete exclusionary interest, though the two legal standards remain distinct.
  • Morrow distinguished: Morrow involved a non-owner licensee holding only a bare right to sue, where the patent owner could moot any suit by granting a royalty-free sublicense; here, the plaintiff is the patent owner and Ingevity's sublicensing right is constrained by consent and royalty obligations that preserve the owner's enforcement interest.

DK Crown Holdings Inc. v. AG 18, LLC

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed, holding that the Board did not abuse its discretion in refusing to consider DK's reply-footnote request to apply Ground 3 (Bryson + Schlottmann) against claim 18 when the petition never asserted Schlottmann against that claim. The court also rejected DK's collateral estoppel argument that invalidation of claims collectively containing all limitations of claim 18 rendered claim 18 invalid.

Disposition: Affirmed

Why it matters: Reinforces that IPR petitioners are strictly bound by the grounds asserted in the petition—post-institution attempts to add new prior art references against a claim, even via footnote, will not be entertained, and collateral estoppel cannot be manufactured by aggregating limitations from separately invalidated claims.

§ 103§ 102waiver / forfeitureclaim preclusionremandnon-precedential
  • DK's IPR petition challenged claim 18 only under Grounds 1 and 2 (Bryson alone); Ground 3 (Bryson + Schlottmann) was directed solely at claims 15–17 in the petition's own summary scorecard.
  • After institution, DK sought in a reply footnote to have the Board analyze claim 18 under Ground 3 'instead of Grounds 1/2'—the Board refused, finding this exceeded proper reply scope by adding a new prior art reference to fill a gap in the petition.
  • The Federal Circuit found no abuse of discretion: the petition contained no cross-reference or indication that Ground 3 or Schlottmann was intended to apply to claim 18, distinguishing Voice Tech Corp. v. Unified Patents (typographical error with substantive cross-reference) on its facts.
  • DK's collateral estoppel theory—that invalidation of claims 10, 12, 15, 16, and 17 collectively covering all limitations of claim 18 estops AG 18 from defending claim 18—was rejected; the Federal Circuit has never applied collateral estoppel by 'cobbling together limitations from disparate claims' to replicate another claim.
  • Opinion is nonprecedential but consistent with and citing precedential authority (Netflix, Inc. v. DivX, LLC; Yita LLC v. MacNeil IP LLC) on the strict petition-scope rule.

DCTOpinionNonprecedential25-1218

TJTM Technologies, LLC v. Google LLC

Holding: Representative claim 1 of U.S. Patent No. 8,958,853 is directed to the abstract idea of 'screening notifications' and recites only generic, well-known components arranged in a conventional manner, failing both steps of the Alice framework. The Federal Circuit affirmed dismissal under § 101.

Disposition: Affirmed

Why it matters: Confirms that adding a user-benefit feature (auto-suppressing phone notifications when paired with a vehicle) to generic mobile hardware does not constitute a technological improvement sufficient to survive § 101 at the pleading stage, and that conclusory inventive-concept allegations cannot defeat a Rule 12(b)(6) motion.

§ 101claim constructioninfringementnon-precedential
  • The '853 patent claims a mobile device that automatically enters 'inactive mode'—suppressing notifications and sending an away message—when paired with a vehicle; the district court and Federal Circuit both characterized this as the abstract idea of 'screening notifications.'
  • Under Alice step one, the court rejected TJTM's analogy to Contour IP and Enfish, holding that improving user experience through a different operational mode does not amount to a technological improvement to the underlying mobile phone technology.
  • Under Alice step two, the court found all claim components (wireless communication module, processor, memory, GUI, away message) generic and well-known, combined in conventional fashion; TJTM's amended complaint offered only conclusory assertions of an inventive concept.
  • At oral argument, TJTM effectively conceded that Bluetooth-style device pairing was known in the field and was not itself an inventive aspect of the claims, undermining its primary step-two argument.

Enviro Tech Chemical Services, Inc. v. Safe Foods Corp.

Holding: The term 'about' as used in the pH range limitation ('about 7.6 to about 10') of the '321 patent is indefinite under § 112(b) because the claims, specification, and prosecution history collectively fail to inform a skilled artisan of the term's scope with reasonable certainty. Disposition: Affirmed

Why it matters: This precedential decision reinforces that 'about' and similar approximation terms are not inherently definite—when a patent's specification contains conflicting examples and prosecution history treats the term inconsistently, particularly where the claimed range was amended to clear prior art that sits close to the range boundary, the term will fail the Nautilus reasonable-certainty standard.
