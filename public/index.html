SLINGSHOT PRINTING LLC v. CANON U.S.A., INC. [OPINION]

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed the PTAB's final written decision that claims 1-5 and 8 of Slingshot's U.S. Patent No. 7,195,341 were unpatentable as obvious. The court held that substantial evidence supported the Board's findings on the Torgerson/Bruce combination and that the Board's reasoning path was sufficiently discernible under the APA.

Disposition: AFFIRMED

Why it matters: The decision is a useful reminder that a finite-number-of-predictable-solutions rationale can survive Federal Circuit review when backed by record evidence of design needs and expert testimony.

PTOPatent Trial and Appeal Board103PTABsubstantial evidenceAPA
  • The patent concerns semiconductor substrate layouts for micro-fluid ejection devices, such as ink-jet printheads.
  • Slingshot challenged the Board's obviousness findings, including the motivation to combine Torgerson and Bruce.
  • The court distinguished Virtek because Canon had developed evidence of known design needs, market pressures, and predictable layout options.
  • The court also rejected Slingshot's APA challenge, finding the Board's explanation reasonably discernible from its citations and analysis.

NETWORK-1 TECHNOLOGIES, INC. v. GOOGLE LLC [OPINION]

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed indefiniteness of Network-1's '988 and '464 patents and affirmed summary judgment of noninfringement for one version of Google's Content ID system, but reversed and remanded as to the Siberia version of Content ID under the '237 patent. The court held that evidence about Siberia's tunable search parameters and Google's internal documents created a genuine fact dispute over whether that version met the claimed sublinear-search limitation.

Disposition: AFFIRMED IN PART, REVERSED IN PART, AND REMANDED

Why it matters: For software-patent litigants, the opinion shows how system-design evidence and internal technical documents can defeat noninfringement summary judgment even when related invalidity rulings remain intact.

DCTDistrict Court112(b)infringementsummary judgmentremandsoftware patents
  • Network-1 accused versions of Google's YouTube Content ID system of infringing patents directed to identifying works and linking traditional media to interactive media.
  • The district court held the '988 and '464 patent claims indefinite and granted Google summary judgment of noninfringement on the '237 patent.
  • The Federal Circuit affirmed noninfringement as to the LSH version of Content ID.
  • The court reversed as to the Siberia version because a factfinder could view the evidence as showing sublinear scaling, including tunable parameters and an internal Google document referring to a need for sublinear search.

DCTOPINIONNonprecedential24-2216

NANTWORKS, LLC v. NIANTIC, INC. [OPINION]

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed that asserted claims of NantWorks' U.S. Patent Nos. 10,664,518 and 10,403,051 were invalid under Section 101. The court agreed that the augmented-reality claims were directed to abstract ideas implemented with conventional components and that NantWorks failed to identify a claimed inventive concept.

Disposition: AFFIRMED

Why it matters: The decision reinforces that AR and location-context claims remain vulnerable under Alice when the alleged improvement is functional or described in the specification but not captured as a concrete claimed technological advance.

DCTDistrict Court101summary judgmentsoftware patents
  • NantWorks accused Niantic's augmented-reality games, including Pokemon Go and Harry Potter: Wizards Unite, of infringing AR-related patents.
  • The district court held asserted claims of the '518 patent invalid on the pleadings and asserted claims of the '051 patent invalid on summary judgment.
  • The Federal Circuit found the claims used generic computing components such as servers, repositories, processors, and devices to implement abstract AR concepts.
  • Expert assertions and specification benefits did not create a triable inventive-concept issue because they relied on unclaimed features or restated the abstract idea.

CENTRIPETAL NETWORKS, LLC v. ITC [OPINION]

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed the ITC's no-violation determination in Centripetal's Section 337 investigation against Keysight. After a related PTAB affirmance mooted issues involving the '917 patent, the court held that substantial evidence supported the Commission's finding that Centripetal failed to establish the technical prong of domestic industry for the '370 patent, and that Centripetal waived multiple infringement arguments on appeal.

Disposition: AFFIRMED

Why it matters: The opinion is a practical ITC reminder that domestic-industry proof and appeal preservation can independently decide a Section 337 case, even apart from validity issues.

ITCInternational Trade CommissioninfringementPTABdomestic industrywaiversubstantial evidenceAPA
  • Centripetal alleged Keysight violated Section 337 through imported products accused of infringing the '370, '917, and '526 patents.
  • The Commission found no violation, and the Federal Circuit affirmed.
  • Issues relating to the '917 patent were mooted by the companion affirmance of unpatentability in Centripetal v. Keysight.
  • For the '370 patent, substantial evidence supported the finding that Centripetal's domestic-industry product did not practice required claim limitations.
  • The court also held that Centripetal waived appeal-dispositive infringement challenges to several limitations.

CENTRIPETAL NETWORKS, LLC v. KEYSIGHT TECHNOLOGIES, INC. [OPINION]

Holding: The Federal Circuit affirmed the PTAB's obviousness determination for claims 1-3, 5-13, and 15-20 of Centripetal's U.S. Patent No. 10,193,917, but reversed the Board's determination that claims 4 and 14 were not shown obvious. The court found no reversible error in the Board's claim-construction analysis and held that substantial evidence compelled obviousness of claims 4 and 14 based on Sourcefire's disclosures.

Disposition: AFFIRMED IN PART, REVERSED IN PART

Why it matters: The companion PTAB decision narrows Centripetal's position in related enforcement activity and illustrates the Federal Circuit's willingness to reverse, rather than remand, when only one obviousness answer is supported by substantial evidence.

PTOPatent Trial and Appeal Board103claim constructionPTABsubstantial evidenceremand
  • The '917 patent is directed to rule-based network-threat detection using packet-filtering rules and packet flow log entries.
  • Centripetal appealed the Board's obviousness findings for most challenged claims, while Keysight cross-appealed the Board's finding that claims 4 and 14 had not been shown obvious.
  • The court affirmed obviousness of claims 1-3, 5-13, and 15-20, rejecting Centripetal's claim-construction and substantial-evidence challenges.
  • On the cross-appeal, the court held the Board's findings on claims 4 and 14 lacked substantial evidence and that Keysight's petition and expert evidence showed the relevant Sourcefire disclosures.
  • The court reversed as to claims 4 and 14 rather than remanding for a second record-making opportunity.
